The History

Treaty No. 1 was signed with the understanding that it would last "as long as the sun shines, the grass grows, and the river flows."

Indigenous peoples have lived on Canada for thousands of years, with their own unique cultures, identities, traditions, languages and institutions.


Communities and Nations Involved

Winnipeg, Portage la Prairie, Selkirk, Steinbach, Emerson, and Winkler are communties on Treaty 1. - Winnipeg CTV News

The seven First Nations that are represented by Treaty One Nation are: Peguis First Nation, Sagkeeng First Nation, Brokenhead Ojibway Nation, Roseau River Anishinaabe First Nation, Long Plain First Nation, Sandy Bay Ojibway First Nation and Swan Lake First Nation. - Government of Canada

The community needed a treaty (Treaty 1) because the Canadian government was trying to acquire legal control over territories that were being held at the time by different Native communities living in Manitoba.

Treaty 1 was also used as a supposed act of good will and peace between Natives at the Canadian government.

Historic Terms

Lands set aside for settlers and Indigenous peoples were designated through treaties; 160 acres of land for each family of five or in like proportions for a smaller or larger family. Promises were made to build schools for every community, to pay each "Indian" a meagre annuity of $3 to $5, and to provide some chosen Chiefs, headmen, and council members with commodities like twine, ploughs, boars, and oxen.

The Reality

If you were to read only the federal government's treaty documents, you might get the impression that First Nations were only allowed to continue existing on the lands set aside for them because of the kindness and goodwill of the British Crown and settlers and that Native Americans were to be feared and needed to be subdued. The then-Chiefs, however, had a different meaning of the treaties when they signed the papers, as you find out when you talk to Elders and other knowledge keepers about that time.
